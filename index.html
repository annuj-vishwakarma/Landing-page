What You Should Look For

  • Choose masks that publish wavelength specs (e.g. 630–660nm for red, 415nm for blue) and have supporting clinical evidence or lab testing.
  • Look for masks with close-fitting panels or reflectors that ensure every area of the face receives an even dose of light.
  • Soft, skin-safe materials and reliable safety features (timers, auto-shutoff) make regular use feasible and low-risk.
  • Variable intensity and preset programs allow you to tailor treatments to your skin sensitivity and goals.
  • A user-friendly design with clear controls, rechargeable battery and minimal setup speeds up daily use and improves adherence.

What You Should Avoid

  • Avoid masks using hard, cheap plastics or unknown silicones that trap heat or irritate skin during repeated use.
  • If a product doesn't publish wavelengths or irradiance, there's no way to compare it to clinically proven devices — steer clear.
  • Masks that shift, pinch, or block breathing are impractical — look for ergonomic designs that stay in place comfortably.
  • Complex pairing, hidden settings, or unclear instructions increase frustration and reduce likelihood of consistent use.
